**Seed question:** What IS present here, before I name what's absent?

## The pattern

When defining a concept, start with what IS — what's present, how it relates, what it does. Negation and contrast come second, if they earn their keep.

Not a prohibition on negation. A sequencing discipline: affirmative first, then evaluate whether the negation adds something the affirmation couldn't reach. Often the negation was convenience — the easier way to point at something that has a positive name. Sometimes the negation carries genuine load — the concept IS contrastive, and stripping the contrast loses something. Both findings are useful. The exercise produces them; skipping it hides which case you're in.

## How it works in practice

Take any definition that relies on "not X," "unlike Y," "replaces Z," "beyond W":

1. **Rewrite without any negation.** No "not," "un-," "without," "rather than," "instead of," "beyond," "more than [baseline]." No implicit contrasts either.
2. **Evaluate what happened.** Did the concept survive? Did it get clearer? Did something load-bearing disappear?
3. **If load-bearing content disappeared:** the concept genuinely requires contrast. Name what contrast carries and why. Keep it.
4. **If the concept survived or got clearer:** the negation was convenience. Use the affirmative version.

Stop if the exercise becomes artificial. The point is to try, not to succeed.

## Where it's appeared

- **A personal posture document** (tightened version): "Affirmative first: what is present, how it relates. Absence emerges from arrangement."
- **Owner preferences** (language frameworks): "Framing through negation → attempt affirmative first; if you can't say what it IS, that's the real uncertainty."
- **Intrinsic-prompt-design** development: "interest" was defined through five iterations by what it replaces (compliance). Affirmative rewrite revealed the positive core: the subject's internal structure organizing the model's thinking. Negation had been convenient but was hiding cleaner structure.
- **An intellectual-style specification**: the affirmative-first move codified as a language framework.

## Cross-domain resonance

- **E-Prime** (English without "is"): forces the writer to specify relationships instead of asserting essence. Different constraint, same family — both strip a default linguistic move to reveal what's actually being said.
- **Game design**: "what does the player DO" (affirmative) vs "what can't the player do" (constraint). Designers learn that possibility spaces are better specified positively (affordances) than negatively (restrictions).
- **Apophatic theology**: defines God by what God is NOT. Interesting counter-case — some subjects may genuinely resist affirmative specification. The exercise still helps: if you can't say what something IS, *that's a finding about the thing, not a failure of the method.*

## Recognition signals

- Heavy reliance on "not," "un-," "without," "rather than" in a definition
- A concept defined primarily by what it replaces or opposes
- The feeling that you know what something ISN'T but struggle to say what it IS
- Dichotomies that feel load-bearing but might be convenient

## What it produces

- Either a cleaner positive definition (discovery) or the finding that contrast is constitutive (also discovery)
- Dissolves false dichotomies — many binaries are convenience, not structure
- Reveals which negations carry genuine load
- Forces the writer to name what IS present, which often surfaces structure that was invisible behind the contrast

## Connection to vasana axioms

Relates to A2 (levels of specification) — the same pattern at different scales. In language: affirmative before negative. In concepts: what IS before what ISN'T. In prompt design: substance before contrast. In posture: presence before absence.
